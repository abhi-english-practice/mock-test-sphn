THE HIDDEN CURRENCY OF OFFICE LIFE

A. People care intensely about relative standing at work. Job titles and salaries are useful clues, but they do not tell the whole story. Some workers command deference without any formal authority, while others hold impressive-sounding posts yet are quietly disregarded. Status can therefore be a powerful tool of motivation, though it can also generate pettiness and conflict.

B. One striking illustration comes from research on German fighter pilots in the second world war. The prestige attached to medals appears to have shaped behaviour. As a higher distinction came within reach, pilots’ performance improved markedly, only to subside again once the award had been secured. Recognition, especially when it comes in visible ranked steps, seems able to justify additional effort and risk.

C. Status also affects how people think about career moves. In one study of Italian jobseekers considering retraining, respondents were offered a choice between an information-technology assistant role and a construction-technician role. The two jobs were comparable in pay and entry requirements, yet far more people preferred the IT path. Perceived fit and social standing, rather than remuneration alone, appear to explain the difference.

D. Within organisations, status may encourage prosocial behaviour – but only up to a point. Some employees gain reputational credit from helping others and are therefore inclined to do so. Yet collaboration is often strongest when colleagues are separated by just the right amount of rank: not so close that one becomes a threat, and not so distant that helping feels pointless or odd.

E. The opposite pattern can also be found. Roles that combine authority with low standing seem especially corrosive. Research suggests that people occupying low-status but high-power positions are more prone to conflict and may become gratuitously demeaning toward others, perhaps because power without respect sharpens resentment rather than easing it.

F. Formal organisational charts miss much of this. They record who reports to whom, but not the social esteem attached to different roles or the informal hierarchy that employees actually navigate. Anyone trying to understand why people exert themselves, help one another, quarrel or switch careers needs to take status seriously.

RUSSIA SHOULD NOT BE WELCOME AT THE WORLD’S TOP ART SHOW

The Venice Biennale is often imagined as a refined refuge from the uglier pressures of political life: gardens, pavilions, art and cultivated debate. Yet even in that setting, politics returns. Since Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, cultural institutions across Europe have struggled to decide how far to distance themselves from Russian artists and institutions. Some early reactions were indiscriminate; over time, a more selective approach emerged, in which apolitical Russians were not automatically excluded but representatives and cheerleaders of the state remained unwelcome. That made the Biennale’s decision to allow Russia back particularly controversial.

The writer sees this as a genuine reversal. In 2022, the artists meant to represent Russia withdrew and Ukraine’s plight was prominently acknowledged. Russia was absent in 2024 as well. Now, however, it is due to return, provoking outrage from Ukrainians and diplomats. Three arguments are usually offered for its inclusion, the writer says, and none survives scrutiny.

The first is the familiar claim that art exists above politics. The article dismisses this bluntly. The Biennale is organised through national pavilions; countries choose artists to represent them; and the event itself praises values such as dialogue, openness and freedom – values the writer insists are political through and through. That makes it impossible, in this view, to treat participation as some neutral cultural act. Moreover, Russia’s own state has imprisoned or driven out artists while claiming devotion to artistic freedom.

A second defence rests on time. No country, it is said, can be ostracised forever. Yet this argument gains traction, the writer suggests, because public attention drifts. As wars drag on, other crises crowd in, and fatigue makes accommodation easier. Such reasoning also suits politicians and businessmen eager to restore links. But moral exhaustion, the article argues, is not the same as moral clarity.

The third defence is whataboutery: if other countries are accused of grave wrongdoing, why single Russia out? The writer rejects that too. Hard cases elsewhere do not erase this one. However imperfect the boundary may be, Russia still falls clearly on the wrong side of it. The article’s sharp comparison is that no one invites a serial killer to a garden party.

The organisers argue that the Biennale does not directly control what national pavilions do. The writer is unconvinced. Even without banning participation outright, they could reduce visibility – for example, by excluding Russia from promotional materials. Instead, by defending the decision in the language of anti-censorship and free expression, they may simply ensure that the festival is filled with protests and boycotts. War and politics, the article concludes, will not be kept outside the gates.
